## Two ends, two different projects

Commerce City covers a lot of ground and its two halves have almost nothing in common. The older southern end, near the river and the industrial corridor that gave the city its name, is compact and established — modest post-war housing on smaller lots with trees that have had time to grow. The northern end is barely twenty years old: Reunion, Buffalo Run, and the subdivisions that turned Adams County farmland into a grid of new rooftops, where the sky is enormous and the shade is theoretical.

Almost every project we quote here falls cleanly into one bucket or the other.

## North: build the shade, engineer for the wind

New neighborhoods on former farmland have a specific problem. The decks that came with the houses are structurally fine and functionally unusable from mid-afternoon on, because there's no canopy and won't be for two decades. Meanwhile the same open ground that gives you the view gives the wind a clear run.

So the work up there is built shade that can take a beating: a [pergola](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) with slats sized to the sun angle you actually face, or a solid roof when you also want protection from hail and the storms that build over the plains on summer afternoons. What makes it a northern Commerce City job specifically is the engineering — an exposed-lot cover is pulled upward in a windstorm, so the post bases, connections, and footings get specified for uplift. It's the same detailing our [Erie](/locations/erie) and [Thornton](/locations/thornton) builds get, for the same reason.

The other frequent request is simply a bigger deck. Production builders ship the minimum platform that satisfies the plan; five or ten years in, people want an outdoor room — extended footprint, built-in seating so a crowd isn't dragging chairs around, lighting to hold the evening, and [railing](/services/railings) slim enough to keep the mountain line to the west intact.

## South: honest inspection first

On the older side, the question is almost always whether an existing deck is worth saving. We answer it the same way every time: look at the structure before quoting the surface. The ledger connection where a deck meets the house is where the real failures happen, and on a home that's been through fifty Colorado winters it deserves a proper look.

If the framing is sound, we quote a [re-deck](/services/deck-repair) — a new [capped composite](/services/composite-decks) surface over the structure you already own, for meaningfully less than starting over. If it isn't, we quote a full [replacement](/services/deck-replacement) on code-current framing and explain exactly why. You get that in writing either way, which is the whole point.

## Permits and HOA review

Inside city limits, Commerce City's building division is your counter, including for the northern growth areas. The planned communities up there add architectural review with real standards for material and color, and that review typically sets the schedule more than the building permit does.

We produce the drawings, file both packets, and run inspections through final sign-off — included in the build, never a surprise line item. Our [permit guide](/deck-permits-south-denver-metro) covers how the process generally works across the metro.
