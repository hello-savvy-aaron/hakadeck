## Decks that work on a slope

Lone Tree was built on rolling terrain, and its neighborhoods — RidgeGate, Heritage Hills, Bluffmont — are full of walk-out basements and homes stepped into hillsides. On those lots the deck isn't an accessory; it's the main outdoor living space, and it's often ten or twelve feet off the ground. Elevated builds are structural projects first: engineered footings on slope, beefed-up posts and beams, and railings that meet code at height without blocking the view you bought the lot for. This is some of our favorite work, and the two-tier elevated build below — a stacked walk-out design a few minutes away in Parker — shows the approach.

## Newer homes, different problems

Most of Lone Tree's housing stock is from the 1990s onward, so we replace fewer failed decks here than in older suburbs. Instead, two jobs dominate. The first is the builder-grade upgrade: production builders often ship a minimal pressure-treated deck, and owners come to us five or ten years in to replace it with a [composite deck](/services/composite-decks) sized for how they actually live. The second is the addition — extending a small original deck, adding a lower patio level beneath a walk-out, or putting a [pergola or cover](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) over a space that gets hammered by afternoon sun on Lone Tree's open, young-tree lots. Shade structures matter more here than almost anywhere we build.

## Permits and design review in Lone Tree

Deck work in Lone Tree runs through the city's building division, and the HOAs — especially in RidgeGate and Heritage Hills — run active architectural review with real standards on materials and colors. We handle the full package: engineered drawings for elevated structures, permit submission, HOA paperwork, and inspections. Our [permit guide](/blog/deck-permits-centennial-arapahoe-douglas) covers how Douglas County-area approvals work if you want the detail.

## Why composite fits Lone Tree

South-facing, treeless, and 5,900 feet up: a Lone Tree deck takes as much UV as any surface in the metro. Wood fades and checks fast here. Capped composite — cool-surface lines especially, if you want dark boards — holds its color and never needs staining at altitude.
