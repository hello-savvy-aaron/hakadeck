## Built for how Highlands Ranch actually sits

Highlands Ranch rolls. The master-planned community grew across genuinely hilly terrain from the 1980s through the 2000s, which is why so many of its homes have walk-out or garden-level basements — and why so many of its decks stand a full story or more off the ground. Elevated decks are structural projects first and outdoor rooms second: taller posts, engineered footings, and railing systems that have to be right. That's the work we do most, and the reason Highlands Ranch homeowners find us.

## The HRCA is not optional — and that's fine

Nearly every home in Highlands Ranch sits under the Highlands Ranch Community Association, and the HRCA's architectural review committee looks at any deck, pergola, or patio cover before a single post goes in. Submittal drawings, material and color specs, site plans — we prepare and file all of it as part of every Highlands Ranch build, then run the [Douglas County building permit](/blog/deck-permits-centennial-arapahoe-douglas) and inspections alongside it. Homeowners who've been through HRCA review solo tell us the paperwork was the worst part of their last project. It isn't part of yours; it's part of ours.

## What we replace here

The first generation of Highlands Ranch decks — redwood and cedar builds from the '80s and '90s — is aging out all at once. South-facing spans at 5,900 feet take a beating from sun and freeze-thaw, and a lot of original railings no longer meet code. The typical Haka project here is a full teardown and [capped composite rebuild](/services/composite-decks), often adding a [pergola or covered section](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) so the new deck is usable through more of the year. If the framing turns out to be sound, we'll tell you and quote a [re-deck](/services/deck-repair) instead — the honest answer is cheaper more often than people expect.
