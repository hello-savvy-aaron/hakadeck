## Actual mountain building

Evergreen is where our work stops being suburban. Lots are steep, bedrock sits close to the surface, the snow that lands on a roof in December is still there in February, and the forest that makes the place beautiful is also the thing that makes wildfire a permanent consideration.

Every one of those changes how a deck gets built — which is why a builder who works exclusively on the metro floor is a genuine risk up here, not merely a mismatch. A structure engineered for a Centennial backyard is not the same structure Evergreen requires.

## Fire-wise, by default

A deck on a mountain lot is one of the most ember-exposed parts of the house. Embers settle on the surface, drift into the framing bays underneath, and find whatever's been stacked down there over the years.

So the detailing is deliberate: [composite or mineral-based boards](/services/composite-decks) instead of untreated softwood, framing bays closed or screened so embers can't nest below the deck, no combustible storage designed into the underside, and honest attention to what's growing in the first few feet around the structure. Where your parcel carries specific ignition-resistant requirements, we confirm them with Jefferson County and detail to them before drawing.

We won't claim any deck is fireproof. We will say that these choices meaningfully reduce a known risk, and that they cost far less to build in than to retrofit. It's the same approach we take on the foothills edge in [Golden](/locations/golden) and [Boulder](/locations/boulder), applied to steeper, more wooded ground.

## Snow is the load that governs

On the plains, the design problem for a covered structure is wind pulling upward. Up here it's the opposite: mountain snow loads run well above metro requirements, and a [patio cover or roofed section](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) has to carry a genuinely heavy accumulation that lingers for months.

That means larger beams, more posts, and bigger footings than the same span would require in Denver. It's the most common thing done wrong on mountain covers by builders working outside their usual territory, and it isn't a cosmetic error — it's the kind that fails under two feet of wet spring snow.

## Rock, slope, and access

Shallow bedrock and decomposed granite show up regularly on Evergreen lots, so footings occasionally need drilling and pinning rather than augering. Steep sites mean materials get staged and often moved by hand where no machine can reach. Both affect the schedule and the labor, and both are things we'd far rather establish at the walkthrough than discover on day two.

The reward for all that work is the reason people live here. Elevated decks on mountain lots are the best rooms in the house, and [railing](/services/railings) choice — cable or slim aluminum — is what keeps the trees and the ridgeline in view instead of behind a picket screen.

## Aging mountain decks

Plenty of Evergreen homes carry a deck built decades ago, and mountain weather is harder on a structure than the calendar suggests — sun at elevation, snow sitting on it for months, and moisture cycling through the framing every spring. We inspect before quoting, starting at the ledger where the deck meets the house.

Sometimes the framing is genuinely sound and a [re-deck](/services/deck-repair) in fire-wise composite is the honest, cheaper answer. Sometimes an old structure was never sized for the snow loads it's been carrying, in which case a full [replacement](/services/deck-replacement) isn't an upsell — it's the finding. We put either conclusion in writing.

## Permits through the county

Evergreen is unincorporated Jefferson County, so there's no separate city counter — the county handles your permit, and its mountain requirements differ from what applies down on the plains. Some communities here also run covenants with architectural review.

We produce the drawings to the county's standard, file the [permit](/deck-permits-south-denver-metro), prepare any covenant packet, and manage inspections through final sign-off.
