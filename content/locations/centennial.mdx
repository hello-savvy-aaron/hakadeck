## Your neighbor, not just your contractor

Haka Decks is headquartered in Centennial, in the heart of the greater Denver Tech Center — our shop sits off Easter Lane near Peoria, a few minutes from Centennial Airport. When we build in Willow Creek, Piney Creek, Foxridge, or Smoky Hill, we're working in our own backyard. That matters for more than sentiment: it means we know what Centennial's homes need, we can be on site quickly during a build, and we're still nearby years later if you ever need us.

## What Centennial homes ask of a deck

Most of Centennial's housing stock went up between the 1970s and the 1990s, which means a large share of the decks on those homes are original or second-generation wood builds now reaching the end of their lives. We replace a lot of them. The pattern is consistent: south- and west-facing decks bleached and split by high-altitude sun, fasteners worked loose by freeze-thaw, and framing that predates today's code. Our usual answer is a [capped composite rebuild](/services/composite-decks) on new or reinforced framing — a deck that shrugs off the sun and hail that killed the last one.

Mature trees are the other Centennial signature. Lots here have forty years of growth on them, and a good deck design works around a canopy rather than clearing it. Multi-level layouts, wrapped corners, and integrated shade structures all show up often in our Centennial work.

## Permits and HOAs in Centennial

Deck work in Centennial runs through the city's building division, and most established neighborhoods — Willow Creek, Piney Creek, Foxridge, and their neighbors — have active HOAs with architectural review on top of that. We handle both: drawings, permit submission, HOA paperwork, and inspections are part of every Haka build, not an extra. If you want the background, our [permit guide for Centennial and the surrounding counties](/blog/deck-permits-centennial-arapahoe-douglas) walks through the whole process.

## Composite-first, for Centennial specifically

Centennial sits at about 5,800 feet with 300-plus days of sun, real hail seasons, and 30-degree temperature swings in spring and fall. That climate is exactly why we lead with capped composite — the material math simply works better here than it does at sea level.
