## Big sky, young lots, real wind

Erie grew up fast on open prairie between Boulder and the Weld County farmland, and that setting defines every deck we build here. The houses are mostly new. The lots are generous and almost entirely treeless. The Front Range fills the western horizon. And the wind arrives with nothing in its way.

Our [prairie-view deck](/portfolio/prairie-view-deck) was built right here, and it's the clearest example we have of designing for those conditions at once: a deck oriented to the mountain view, shade built rather than borrowed, and a structure engineered for a lot with no windbreak.

## Shade is a structure, not an accessory

In older suburbs, forty-year-old trees do the shading. Erie doesn't have that yet, and won't for a couple of decades. So on an open lot, usable outdoor space is something you build: a [pergola](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) with slats sized and oriented for the sun angle you actually face, or a solid-roof cover when you want full protection from sun, hail, and the afternoon storms that roll off the mountains.

Get it right and the deck becomes the part of the house people live in from May through October. Skip it and you've built a beautiful surface nobody sits on after two in the afternoon.

## Wind is the engineering problem out here

This is where Erie builds genuinely differ from metro builds. On an exposed prairie lot, a covered structure behaves like a wing — the governing load is uplift, pulling the whole thing upward, not snow pressing down. That drives engineered post-base connections, footings sized for the exposure, and hardware specified accordingly.

It's the same principle we apply on [Castle Pines](/locations/castle-pines) ridges and in [Golden](/locations/golden), and it's why a properly engineered cover costs more than a bargain bid. It's also why ours are still there after the windstorms that take the bargain ones apart.

## Keep the view you paid for

Erie's western sightlines are the reason a lot of these lots sold. That makes [railing](/services/railings) a design decision rather than a code checkbox — cable and slim aluminum systems hold the Front Range skyline where traditional pickets slice it into strips. On walk-out and elevated builds, the railing choice usually affects the finished feeling of the deck more than the boards do.

Because most Erie homes are recent, a good share of our work here starts with a builder-grade deck: the minimum platform a production builder shipped, now five or ten years old and far too small for how the house lives. Sometimes that structure is sound enough to carry a new composite surface, which makes a [re-deck](/services/deck-repair) the honest quote; sometimes it's better to start over with a proper [replacement](/services/deck-replacement) sized for the yard. We inspect before we say which.

For the surface itself we lead with [capped composite](/services/composite-decks). Erie sits in the same high-UV, hard-hail, freeze-thaw band as the rest of the northern metro, and an unshaded prairie deck takes all three with no canopy to soften them. Composite's cap handles it with a hose and soap; wood out here starts an annual refinishing clock. Our [materials comparison](/composite-vs-hardwood-decking-colorado) lays out both honestly.

## Two counties, one town counter

Erie straddles the Boulder–Weld county line, which routinely surprises new homeowners. Permits inside town limits run through the Town of Erie either way, but your county still matters for parcel-level details — easements, mapping, what's on your plat. We confirm both before drawing, produce the drawings, pull the [permit](/deck-permits-south-denver-metro), and manage inspections. Most Erie communities also run HOA architectural review; we prepare that packet too.
