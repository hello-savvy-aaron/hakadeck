## Littleton's decks are due

Littleton is one of the metro's older suburbs — much of its housing went up from the 1960s through the 1980s, and a striking number of those homes still carry their original or second wood deck. At Colorado altitude, a forty-year-old redwood deck isn't a charming original feature; it's a liability with splinters. The bulk of our Littleton work is honest replacement: tear out the tired structure, rebuild in [capped composite](/services/composite-decks) on code-current framing, and give the house an outdoor room that outlasts the roof. When the old framing is genuinely sound, we say so and quote a [re-deck](/services/deck-repair) instead.

The tell is rarely dramatic. It's a slight give underfoot near the house, a railing that moves more than it should, or fasteners backing out along a south-facing edge. By the time those show up the ledger connection usually deserves a look, and that's the part of a deck that fails expensively. Our [deck replacement](/services/deck-replacement) work almost always starts with a free structural inspection and a straight answer about which of the three options — repair, re-deck, replace — you're actually looking at.

## Which Littleton are you in?

Here's the local wrinkle nobody warns you about: "Littleton" is far bigger than the City of Littleton, and your mailing address doesn't tell you who issues your permit.

Depending on where you actually sit, a Littleton deck permit may run through the City of Littleton, unincorporated **Arapahoe, Jefferson, or Douglas County**, or one of the separately incorporated towns tucked inside the postal boundary — **Columbine Valley** and **Bow Mar** both run their own town governments. Ken Caryl addresses add a substantial HOA architectural review on the Jefferson County side, and Roxborough sits in Douglas County entirely. Six possible counters, no shared paperwork, different submittal standards, different review calendars.

Two more things worth checking before a layout gets locked: older Littleton lots frequently carry irrigation-ditch easements — the historic City Ditch runs through this part of the valley — plus the usual utility and drainage easements, and properties near the South Platte can fall inside mapped floodplain. Both affect where a structure can legally go.

We sort all of it out. We confirm the jurisdiction, check your plat and the mapping during design, produce drawings to the right standard, pull the [permit](/blog/deck-permits-centennial-arapahoe-douglas), and manage the inspections wherever they run. You never stand at a counter, and you never find out in week three that you drew the deck over an easement.

## Designed around the canopy, not against it

What makes Littleton yards special is the growth on them — neighborhoods like The Village, Southbridge, Bow Mar South, and the blocks off the historic downtown have mature trees that new-build suburbs won't match for decades. Good deck design here works with that canopy: wrapped corners, split levels that step around trunks, and shade structures that fill the gaps the trees don't cover. Our tongue-and-groove [patio cover in Littleton](/portfolio/patio-cover-tongue-groove) is a favorite example — an exposed slab turned into a genuine outdoor room.

Where the canopy doesn't reach, we build the shade. A [pergola or patio cover](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) over a west-facing section is the difference between a deck that's beautiful and a deck that's usable at six on a July evening, and slim [railing systems](/services/railings) keep the sightlines through the trees rather than fencing them off.

## Built for the way Littleton actually uses a yard

Littleton is an outdoors-first town in a way that shows up in what people ask us for. Downtown Main Street, Hudson Gardens, South Platte Park and the Carson Nature Center, the trail system along the river, Chatfield at the south end — the whole city is organized around being outside, and the summer calendar follows. Graduation season fills backyards across the Littleton Public Schools attendance areas, Western Welcome Week takes over Main Street in August, and the stretch in between is one long entertaining season.

That's why so much of our Littleton work is really outdoor-room work rather than plain platforms: seating built into the perimeter so a crowd isn't hauling chairs, a shaded section that stays comfortable through an afternoon party, lighting so the evening doesn't end at sundown, and — often enough — an [outdoor kitchen](/services/outdoor-kitchens) so the cook isn't stuck inside missing it. Design for the eight days a year you host, and the deck is better on the other 357 too.
