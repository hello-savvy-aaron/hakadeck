## Weld County proportions

Greeley has never had to be stingy with space, and that shapes the work. Lots run generous, the horizon is wide open in most directions, and the newer neighborhoods on the edges of the city were laid out on farmland with the same relaxed relationship to acreage that the surrounding county has always had.

For deck design that's an opportunity and a trap in equal measure. The opportunity is obvious — you can build what the house actually wants rather than what fits between the setbacks. The trap is proportion: a standard suburban platform dropped onto a Greeley lot reads as undersized, like furniture borrowed from a smaller house.

## Build zones, not a platform

The projects that work here are usually zoned. A covered section where cooking and eating happen, an open section for the people who came out for the sun, and frequently a lower level stepping down toward the yard instead of one flat plane floating above it.

[Built-in seating](/services/deck-replacement) earns its keep on lots like these, because people gather in numbers and nobody wants to carry chairs across a yard this size. Lighting matters just as much — the long evenings are the point of having the space. And for the surface itself, [capped composite](/services/composite-decks) is the straightforward answer: an unshaded deck out here takes full high-altitude UV all day, serious hail seasons, and sharp freeze-thaw swings with no canopy to soften any of it.

## Wind is the design load

There's a lot of open farmland upwind of most Greeley neighborhoods, and while the deck surface doesn't care, anything with a roof over it does. A [pergola or solid patio cover](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) on exposed ground is pulled upward in a windstorm rather than pressed down by snow — so post bases, connections, and footings all get specified for uplift.

Solid roofs catch more wind than open pergolas and need it more. This is the same engineering we bring to [Brighton's](/locations/brighton) big lots and [Erie's](/locations/erie) prairie, and it's the honest explanation for why a properly engineered cover costs more than the cheapest number you'll be quoted.

## Older neighborhoods, honest inspections

Greeley's established core has housing old enough that its decks are usually second-generation and overdue. Our approach doesn't change: inspect the structure before quoting the surface, starting with the ledger connection where the deck meets the house, because that's where the expensive failures live.

If the framing is genuinely sound, a [re-deck](/services/deck-repair) puts a new composite surface on the structure you already own for meaningfully less. If it isn't, a full [replacement](/services/deck-replacement) on code-current framing is the right call and we'll explain exactly why. Written either way — including when the cheaper answer wins.

Where a lot opens west, [railing](/services/railings) choice is worth the spend: slim aluminum or cable keeps the Front Range on the horizon instead of behind a fence of pickets.

## City or county

Inside city limits the City of Greeley is your counter; outside them it's Weld County, and the two aren't interchangeable. Given how many properties around Greeley sit close to that boundary, confirming the parcel first is genuinely worth the ten minutes. We do that, then produce drawings to the right standard, file the [permit](/deck-permits-south-denver-metro), and manage inspections through sign-off.
