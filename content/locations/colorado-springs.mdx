## Higher, harder weather, better mountain

Colorado Springs is the southern end of our range and, in some ways, the most demanding place we build. At roughly 6,035 feet it sits a few hundred feet above Denver, with stronger UV, sharper freeze-thaw swings, and the Palmer Divide overhead generating weather that behaves like nowhere else on the Front Range. Then there's the hail, which arrives often enough and hard enough to run an entire regional roofing industry.

And it has Pikes Peak. Whatever else a deck here needs to do, it needs to not get in the way of that.

## Hail is the material argument

Every region has a climate problem that decides the material conversation. Up in the metro it's altitude UV. Here it's hail, and it isn't close.

Hail is brutal on stained and painted wood — it pits the finish, dents softer boards, and sends a deck into a sand-and-restain cycle years earlier than it should. A [capped composite](/services/composite-decks) board takes those strikes on a polymer cap engineered for exactly this kind of abuse, and the [brands we're certified to install](/blog/trex-vs-timbertech-vs-fiberon) warranty the surface for decades. The same logic applies overhead: if you want a [patio cover](/services/pergolas-patio-covers), build the roof to take a hailstorm rather than picking the thinnest panel on the shelf.

We'll still build in real wood for homeowners who want it — our [materials comparison](/composite-vs-hardwood-decking-colorado) plays both sides straight — we're just honest about the maintenance clock that starts at this elevation, in this hail belt.

## Permits run through Pikes Peak Regional

Here's the local mechanic that surprises anyone who's built in Denver: Colorado Springs doesn't run its own separate building counter the way metro cities do. Permits for the city and the surrounding parts of El Paso County go through the **Pikes Peak Regional Building Department**, a shared regional authority. It's a different system with different submittal expectations, and many neighborhoods — Flying Horse, Wolf Ranch, Cordera, Briargate and the newer communities especially — layer HOA architectural review on top.

We produce the drawings, run the submittal through PPRBD, prepare the HOA packet where one applies, and manage inspections. You never work out who to call. If you want the general shape of how deck permitting works, our [permit guide](/deck-permits-south-denver-metro) covers the process, and the Springs-specific handling is ours.

## Fire-wise where the trees start

On the wooded edges — Black Forest, the western foothill neighborhoods, anywhere the tree line meets the subdivision — a deck is one of the most ember-exposed parts of a house. We detail for that: non-combustible or ignition-resistant surfaces, framing bays closed or screened so embers can't nest underneath, and an underside that isn't designed as kindling storage. Where your parcel carries specific requirements, we confirm them before detailing. It's the same approach we take on foothills lots in [Golden](/locations/golden).

## Design around the Peak

The best Colorado Springs decks are organized around a sightline. That makes [railing](/services/railings) a real design decision — cable and slim aluminum systems keep Pikes Peak in view where traditional pickets chop it into slices — and it makes orientation worth arguing about at the design stage. Add [built-in seating](/services/deck-replacement) and lighting and the deck becomes the room the house is actually organized around, which at this elevation is usable far more of the year than newcomers expect.
