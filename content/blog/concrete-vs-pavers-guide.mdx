---It's the most common question we get on patio projects: poured concrete or pavers? The marketing on both sides is loud, the price quotes can be surprisingly close, and the right answer depends on factors most homeowners don't think about until after the project is done. This guide gets specific on cost, lifespan, maintenance, and the climate realities that matter in Colorado.

## What You're Actually Choosing Between

**Poured concrete** is one continuous slab. It's mixed on-site, poured into forms, finished smooth or with a texture, optionally stamped or stained. Once it cures, it's a single monolithic surface.

**Pavers** are individual units — concrete, brick, or natural stone — set on a compacted gravel and sand base, with sand swept into the joints. The surface flexes as a system rather than acting as one solid piece.

Both can look great. Both can fail badly when installed poorly. The difference is in how they behave over time.

## Cost: Closer Than You Think

Installed prices in our market for a typical residential patio:

| Surface | Installed Cost / sq ft | Lifespan |
|---|---|---|
| Standard broom-finish concrete | $8–$14 | 25–30 yrs |
| Stamped / colored concrete | $12–$22 | 25–30 yrs |
| Concrete pavers | $14–$24 | 30–50 yrs |
| Brick pavers | $18–$28 | 50+ yrs |
| Natural stone pavers (flagstone, travertine) | $22–$45 | 50–100 yrs |

A 400 sq ft patio runs roughly $3,200–$5,600 in standard concrete versus $5,600–$9,600 in concrete pavers. Stamped concrete and entry-level pavers often quote within a few hundred dollars of each other.

## Where Concrete Wins

**Lower upfront cost** for plain or lightly-finished installs. If budget is the deciding factor, concrete usually wins by 20–40%.

**Faster install.** A typical residential patio is poured in a day, walkable in 24–48 hours, fully cured in 28 days. Pavers take 2–4 days of skilled labor for the same area.

**Smooth, continuous surface.** Better for furniture stability, easier for someone using a walker or wheelchair, no joints to catch heels or table legs.

**Fewer weeds.** No joints, no place for seeds to land.

**Modern aesthetic.** Plain or polished concrete fits contemporary architecture in a way pavers don't.

## Where Pavers Win

**Cracks don't ruin the project.** This is the big one in Colorado. Concrete *will* crack — every concrete patio over a few years old has cracks somewhere. Properly placed control joints manage where, but they don't prevent it. Pavers, by design, don't crack as a system because the joints absorb movement.

**Repairable.** A damaged paver gets pulled and replaced in 20 minutes. A cracked concrete slab gets patched (visible) or replaced (expensive).

**No long curing window.** Walk on them the day they're installed.

**Permeable options.** Permeable pavers let stormwater drain through to the soil — increasingly important for HOAs and municipal codes restricting impervious surface coverage.

**Better resale value, generally.** Real estate appraisers and agents in our market consistently value a paver patio higher than the equivalent concrete one.

**More design flexibility.** Patterns, borders, color blends, integrated fire pits and seat walls. Concrete can be stamped to mimic this, but stamped concrete is the most failure-prone form of concrete patio there is — the texture wears, the color fades, and the cracks still come.

## The Colorado-Specific Reality

Our climate is the worst-case scenario for concrete. Freeze-thaw cycles drive water into hairline cracks, expand it, and widen them every winter. Expansive clay soils along the Front Range shift with moisture, cracking slabs that weren't poured with adequate reinforcement and joint spacing.

Pavers handle this far better because they flex. A properly built paver patio will look essentially the same in year 20 as it did in year 1. A concrete patio in the same location will have visible cracks, possibly settling, and likely some surface scaling from de-icer exposure.

This doesn't mean concrete is wrong here — it means concrete in Colorado *must* be poured at proper thickness (4 inches minimum, 5 inches for vehicle loads), with reinforcement (rebar or fiber-mesh), with control joints spaced no more than 8–10 feet apart, on properly compacted base. Cutting any of those corners will save money up front and cost more in year 5.

## Maintenance Reality Check

**Concrete:** Re-seal every 2–3 years if stamped or stained, every 5 years if plain. Repair cracks as they appear. Replace de-icer with sand in winter (de-icers eat the surface).

**Pavers:** Re-sand the joints every 2–4 years (polymeric sand makes this easier — it hardens between paver edges and resists weeds). Pull and reset any individual pavers that settle. Optional sealer every 3–5 years.

Both require attention. Pavers reward small annual maintenance. Concrete tends to be ignored until it has a real problem.

## A Quick Decision Framework

**Choose concrete if:**
- Budget is the dominant constraint
- You want a modern, minimalist look
- The patio is in a sheltered location (under a covered porch, for example) where freeze-thaw exposure is limited
- You're okay with eventual cracks and don't plan to sell soon

**Choose pavers if:**
- You're staying in the home long-term and want a surface that looks new for decades
- The patio is in full sun or freeze-thaw exposure
- You want design complexity (curves, patterns, integrated features)
- Resale value matters
- Your soil is reactive clay (common along the Front Range)

**Avoid stamped concrete unless** you're committed to re-sealing every 2 years and accepting that the texture will wear and the color will fade.

## The Bottom Line

For a Colorado homeowner weighing the long view, pavers are usually the better investment despite the higher upfront cost. For a tight budget, a simple, well-built broom-finish concrete patio is honest, durable, and looks fine. The wrong answer for almost everyone is poorly-installed cheap concrete or poorly-installed cheap stamped concrete — that's where most homeowner regret lives.
