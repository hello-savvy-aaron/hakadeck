"Best deck builder near me" turns up a page of five-star reviews and confident-sounding homepages, and none of it tells you much. Reviews can be genuine and still miss the thing that actually matters on your project. Here's what we'd actually check, working from the signals that are real and verifiable rather than the ones that just sound good.

## Licensed and insured — and willing to prove it

Every legitimate contractor will say they're licensed and insured. The distinction is whether they'll produce the certificate without hesitation. A deck sits on your property for decades and involves permits, inspections, and real structural risk — if something goes wrong mid-build, the contractor's coverage is what stands between the incident and your own homeowner's policy. Ask for the certificate of insurance before you sign anything, not after.

## Manufacturer certification tiers, and what they actually unlock

Composite and PVC decking brands run installer certification programs — Trex Platinum Pro, Deckorators Pro Elite, TimberTech authorized dealer, and similar tiers from other manufacturers. These aren't marketing badges. They're the manufacturer's own assessment of installation quality, and on some product lines the tier directly extends the labor-coverage period of your warranty — the part of the warranty that pays for reinstallation, not just replacement boards. A contractor who can't name their certification tier probably doesn't have one, which means you're relying entirely on their own workmanship warranty with no manufacturer backing behind the installation itself.

## A written workmanship warranty with a real term

"We stand behind our work" isn't a warranty; it's a sentence. Ask for the term in writing — one year, two years, longer — and what it actually covers: framing, fasteners, and connections, or just the surface. Then ask how a claim has actually played out for a past customer. A contractor who's proud of their warranty will tell you the story; one who's never had to honor it might not have one worth much.

## Who pulls the permit

This is the fastest way to separate a professional operation from a handshake crew. A permitted deck means a third party — the city or county building department — has independently checked the footings, framing, and railings against code. An unpermitted deck is a standing liability and a documented red flag on a home inspection when you sell. If a contractor suggests skipping the permit "to save time," that's not a shortcut; it's a sign nothing on the job has been checked by anyone but them. [What Front Range permits actually involve](/deck-permits-south-denver-metro) is worth reading regardless of who you hire.

## An itemized bid, not a lump sum

A single number — "$22,000, all in" — can't be compared to anything and gives a contractor nowhere honest to put a change order. An itemized bid breaks out plans and permits, demolition, footings, framing, decking, railing, and stairs, so you can hold two bids side by side and see exactly where they differ. It's also the only format that tells you whether "the cheap bid" is actually cheaper, or just missing a line item the other one included. Our [cost guide](/deck-cost-guide-denver) shows what a real itemized bid looks like, line by line.

## Local specialization, not just local address

A contractor can be headquartered five minutes from your house and still build decks the way they'd build them in Phoenix or Charlotte. What matters is whether the structure is actually engineered for where you live: footings set below the Front Range frost line, framing sized for real snow load, hardware and fasteners specified for freeze-thaw, and — if there's a roof involved — connections engineered for wind uplift rather than copied from a flatland detail. Ask what changes about their build for Colorado specifically. A vague answer is the answer.

## Reviews you can actually verify

A five-star average is a start, not a finding. Look for reviews with enough detail that you could verify the project happened — a neighborhood mentioned, a specific type of build, a timeline. Better still, ask for two or three addresses of recent local work you can drive past, and permission to knock on a door and ask a real homeowner how the year since the build has gone. Reputable builders offer this without flinching.

## The one-year test

The best signal of all is the one almost nobody thinks to ask about: does the builder come back after the first winter? A deck's real test in Colorado isn't the day it's finished — it's the first hard freeze-thaw cycle, the first heavy snow load, the first summer of full UV. A contractor who checks back a year later, unprompted, is telling you something about how they expect the work to hold up. One who disappears after the final payment is telling you something too.

## Put it in writing before you sign

None of this is about picking the biggest name or the flashiest truck wrap. It's about a handful of things you can actually verify: a real insurance certificate, a named certification tier, a warranty term in writing, a permitted process, an itemized bid, and a builder willing to talk about what changes for your specific climate.
