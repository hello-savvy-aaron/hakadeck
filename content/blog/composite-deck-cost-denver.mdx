---Homeowners across Centennial, Highlands Ranch, and Lone Tree ask us the same first question: *what's a composite deck going to run me?* The honest answer is that it depends on size, height, and how the yard sits — but "it depends" is a useless answer when you're trying to budget. So here are real 2026 numbers for the south Denver metro, what moves them, and how to read a bid so you know what you're actually paying for.

## The short answer

For a professionally built capped-composite deck in the Denver metro, expect **$40–$70 per square foot installed** in 2026. That's the all-in number — materials, substructure, fasteners, labor, permits, and cleanup.

Run against common deck sizes, that works out to roughly:

| Deck size | Typical installed cost |
|---|---|
| 200 sq ft (small, single-level) | $8,000–$14,000 |
| 350 sq ft (average backyard deck) | $14,000–$24,500 |
| 500 sq ft (large or multi-use) | $20,000–$35,000 |
| 700+ sq ft (multi-level / wraparound) | $30,000–$55,000+ |

Denver runs a little above the national average, and there's a real reason for it: our builds carry heavier structural requirements than decks in milder climates (more on that below).

## Where the money actually goes

People assume the boards are most of the cost. They're not. On a typical composite deck, the surface material is only about **a third** of the total. The rest breaks down roughly like this:

- **Substructure (30–40%)** — footings, posts, beams, and joists. This is the part nobody sees and the part that determines whether your deck is still solid in 20 years. In Colorado this is where cost gets added, not saved.
- **Decking boards (30–35%)** — the composite surface and matching fascia.
- **Labor (25–30%)** — framing, board install, and finish work.
- **Railings, stairs, permits, and extras** — the variable line items that swing a bid the most.

## What drives your number up or down

**Height and stairs.** A ground-level deck is far cheaper than a walkout or second-story deck. Every foot off the ground adds footings, taller posts, guardrail requirements, and often a full run of stairs — a single staircase can add $1,500–$4,000.

**Railing choice.** This is the single biggest swing after size. Basic composite railing is economical; cable rail, aluminum, or glass panels can add $70–$150+ per linear foot. On a mid-size deck, railing selection alone can move the total by $5,000 or more.

**Board tier.** Entry composite (Trex Enhance, TimberTech Edge) sits at the low end. Premium capped and PVC lines (TimberTech AZEK, Deckorators Voyage) push the top end — worth it for cooler surface temps and longer warranties, which matter in our sun. We break down the tradeoffs in our [Trex vs. TimberTech vs. Fiberon comparison](/blog/trex-vs-timbertech-vs-fiberon).

**Site conditions.** Sloped lots (common in Castle Rock, Ken Caryl, and the foothills-adjacent neighborhoods), rocky soil, and hard-to-reach backyards all add labor and engineering.

**Shape and levels.** Rectangles are cheap. Angles, curves, multiple tiers, and picture-frame borders all add cutting, waste, and time.

## Why Denver decks cost a bit more to build right

Colorado's climate isn't a marketing line — it's a line item. A deck here has to survive high-altitude UV, summer hail, and a freeze-thaw cycle that works fasteners loose and heaves poorly-set footings. Built correctly, that means:

- **Deeper footings** poured below the frost line (typically 30–36 inches in our metro) so the deck doesn't shift as the ground freezes and thaws.
- **Corrosion-rated hardware and hidden fasteners** that won't back out over years of thermal movement.
- **Proper joist spacing and flashing** so water and snowmelt drain instead of pooling against the house.

A cheaper bid that skips these isn't a better deal — it's a deck that needs rework in five years. When you compare quotes, the substructure spec is where you find out who's actually building for this climate. Our [composite deck page](/services/composite-decks) walks through how we frame for it.

## How to read a composite deck bid

A real bid should spell out, at minimum:

1. **Board brand, line, and color** — not just "composite."
2. **Substructure material and footing depth** — pressure-treated vs. steel, and how deep the footings go.
3. **Railing type and linear footage.**
4. **Whether permits and engineering are included** (they should be).
5. **Warranty terms** — both the manufacturer's on the boards and the builder's on the labor.

If a quote is a single lump-sum number with none of that detail, you can't compare it to anything, and you can't hold anyone to it. Push for the line items.

## Composite vs. wood — is the premium worth it here?

Upfront, composite costs more than pressure-treated pine or cedar. Over the life of the deck, that gap narrows and usually flips — composite skips the every-other-year stain-and-seal cycle that Colorado sun forces on wood, and it doesn't gray, splinter, or check the way exposed lumber does at altitude. We ran the full 10-year math in our [composite vs. wood guide](/blog/composite-vs-natural-decking-guide). For most homes in our service area, composite is the material we recommend — and it's not close.

## The honest bottom line

Budget **$14,000–$25,000** for a typical backyard composite deck in the south Denver metro, more if you're going tall, large, multi-level, or into premium railings. The cheapest bid is rarely the best value here, because the savings almost always come out of the substructure — the one part you can't see and can't easily fix later.
