Every April, our phone starts ringing with the same sentence: *"We'd love to have the deck done by summer."* And every April, we have to explain the same math — a deck signed in late spring is usually a deck finished in mid-summer. The homeowners lounging on new composite by Memorial Day are the ones who called us in January.

So here's the honest answer to when you should build a deck in Colorado — and why it's almost certainly earlier in the year than you think.

## The short answer

**Decks can be built nearly year-round in Colorado. The real bottleneck isn't weather — it's everything that happens before the crew shows up.** Permits and HOA approvals run two to four weeks in most Front Range jurisdictions, design takes a conversation or two, and materials have lead times. Add it up and the calendar looks like this:

| You sign in… | You're using the deck by… |
|---|---|
| November–January | Early–mid spring |
| February–March | Late spring / Memorial Day |
| April–May | Mid-summer |
| June–July | Late summer |
| August–October | Fall — with the best build weather of the year |

Most builds take three to ten days on site once we break ground. The other 90% of the timeline is paperwork and planning — which is why *when you start* matters far more than *when we build*.

## Season by season, builder's honest version

### Fall: the sleeper pick

September and October might be the best deck-building weather Colorado offers — stable temperatures, low humidity, hail season winding down, and ground that's still easy to excavate for footings. Scheduling is more flexible than the summer crush, and you get a full winter of the permit-and-design process happening while you're not in a hurry. A deck finished in October is also a deck that's ready on the *first* nice day of spring, not the fiftieth.

### Winter: yes, really

We build through Colorado winters, and [capped composite](/services/composite-decks) is a big part of why. Composite doesn't need a staining window — there's no finish waiting on warm, dry weather the way a wood deck's oil or sealer does. Front Range winters are also drier than most of the country's springs; between storms we get long stretches of workable weather. Footings already go below the frost line on every build regardless of season, and cold-weather concrete practice is routine for us. The trade-off is honest: an occasional snow-day pause. The upside is real: the most flexible scheduling of the year, and a finished deck before the spring rush even begins.

### Spring: the rush

Spring is when everyone calls — which means permit offices are at their busiest, build calendars fill fastest, and the weather is at its least cooperative. March and April snow, May mud, and the start of hail season all visit the job site. We build plenty of great decks in spring; you'll just wait longer for the same result. If your target is "deck by summer," spring is when the deck should be *under construction*, not when the first phone call happens.

### Summer: great decks, peak demand

Summer builds are straightforward — long days, warm nights for concrete, predictable afternoon-storm rhythms we plan around. It's also peak season, so lead times stretch. And here's the part nobody tells you: a deck finished in August gives you a few weekends of use before fall. The same deck signed the previous winter would have given you the whole season.

## The permit math nobody does

Whatever the season, the approvals pipeline runs at its own speed. Between city or county [permits and HOA architectural review](/blog/deck-permits-centennial-arapahoe-douglas), most Front Range projects carry two to four weeks of lead time before a post hole gets dug — longer in spring when the counters are slammed. We handle all of that paperwork on every build, and we order materials during the wait so the crew hits the ground running the day approvals land. But we can't compress a review committee's meeting schedule, and neither can anyone else. Starting early is the only cheat code there is.

## Does the season change the price?

Materials cost the same in January as they do in June. What changes is scheduling pressure — off-season projects get more calendar flexibility and first pick of build slots, and you're never paying a premium to squeeze into a packed month. If you're putting numbers together, our [composite deck cost guide](/blog/composite-deck-cost-denver) has real 2026 ranges for the Denver metro, and the [deck cost calculator](/deck-cost-calculator) will give you a planning figure in thirty seconds.

## One more Colorado-specific reason to build off-season

Hail. The Front Range's hail season runs roughly May through September — squarely over the traditional "deck season." A capped composite deck doesn't care when it was built; the polymer cap takes hail strikes year-round. But if your project includes a [pergola or solid-roof cover](/services/pergolas-patio-covers), having it standing *before* hail season starts means the deck furniture, the grill, and the outdoor room underneath are protected for the whole summer instead of the back half of it.

## The bottom line

The best time to build a deck in Colorado is fall or winter — and the best time to *start planning* one is right now, whatever the month, because the paperwork is the longest part of the project. Sign in the off-season and you'll be the neighbor relaxing on a finished deck in May while everyone else is still waiting on permits.
