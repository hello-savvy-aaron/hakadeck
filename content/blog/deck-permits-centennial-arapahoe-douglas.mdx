---"Do I really need a permit for a deck?" It's one of the first questions homeowners ask, usually hoping the answer is no. In the Denver metro, the answer is almost always **yes** — and pulling the permit is what protects you at resale, with your insurer, and against a deck that isn't built to survive Colorado's freeze-thaw. Here's how permitting actually works across Centennial, Arapahoe County, and Douglas County in 2026, so you know what you're signing up for.

> **Fast disclaimer:** codes and fees change, and every jurisdiction has its own quirks. Treat this as an orientation, not the final word — always confirm current requirements with your building department (or let us handle it). ## Do you need a permit?

In practically every jurisdiction in our service area, you need a building permit for a deck if **any** of these are true — which covers nearly all real decks:

- It's attached to the house.
- It's more than 30 inches above grade at any point.
- It exceeds a small area threshold (often around 200 sq ft).
- It includes a roof, cover, or pergola attached to the structure.

A tiny, freestanding, ground-level platform *might* be exempt — but "ground level" and "small" are defined more narrowly than people assume, and getting it wrong is expensive. When in doubt, permit it.

## Why permitting is actually in your favor

It's tempting to skip the paperwork, but an un-permitted deck creates real problems:

- **Resale.** Buyers' inspectors and title work flag un-permitted structures. It can kill or delay a sale, or force you to tear out and rebuild to code before closing.
- **Insurance.** If an un-permitted deck fails and someone's hurt, your claim can be denied.
- **Safety.** Colorado's frost line and freeze-thaw cycle are exactly why inspections exist. The footing-depth and guardrail checks are the difference between a deck that lasts 25 years and one that heaves or fails.

A permit isn't red tape — it's a third party confirming your deck was built to survive the climate it's standing in.

## What the process looks like

Whether you or your builder pulls it, the flow is broadly the same:

1. **Application + plans.** Submit a site plan and structural details — footing depth, framing, beam and joist sizing, guardrail and stair specs. Larger or elevated decks may need an engineer's stamp.
2. **Plan review.** The building department checks the design against current code (jurisdictions in our area are on recent IRC editions with local amendments).
3. **Permit issued + fees paid.** Fees typically scale with project value.
4. **Inspections during the build.** Expect at least a **footing/foundation inspection** (before concrete is poured) and a **final inspection**. Some jurisdictions add a framing inspection in between.
5. **Final sign-off.** The deck is now on record as code-compliant — the paper trail that protects you at resale.

The footing inspection is the big one in Colorado: inspectors verify the holes hit the required depth **below the frost line** (generally 30–36 inches locally) so the deck doesn't shift as the ground freezes and thaws. This is the single most common thing DIY and cut-rate builds get wrong.

## Centennial

Centennial handles its own building permits through its Community Development department. Deck permits require a site plan and structural details, footing and final inspections, and adherence to Centennial's adopted building code and any zoning setbacks. If your property sits in an HOA — common across Centennial — you'll typically need **HOA architectural approval in addition to** the city permit. The two are separate approvals; getting one doesn't waive the other.

## Arapahoe County (unincorporated)

If your home is in **unincorporated** Arapahoe County rather than an incorporated city, permitting runs through the county's Public Works & Development building division. Same essentials: structural plans, footings below frost line, guardrail and stair compliance, footing and final inspections. Setback and zoning rules depend on your specific lot and district.

## Douglas County (Highlands Ranch, Lone Tree area, unincorporated)

Douglas County — covering much of the fast-growing south metro including unincorporated areas near Highlands Ranch and the Lone Tree/Castle Pines corridor — permits decks through its Building Division. Note that **incorporated towns like Castle Rock, Lone Tree, and Parker run their own building departments**, so which office you deal with depends on your exact address. HOA architectural review is nearly universal in these newer subdivisions and is a separate step from the county permit.

## City & County of Denver

Inside Denver proper, deck permits run through Community Planning & Development. Denver's process has a couple of quirks the suburbs don't: low, unattached decks below Denver's size and height thresholds can sometimes skip a full permit (verify before assuming — the thresholds are specific), while attached or elevated decks need plans, permit, and inspections like anywhere else. Denver's older housing stock also means more landmark districts and zoning overlays, which can add review steps in designated neighborhoods.

## What about pergolas and patio covers?

Roofed and shade structures are usually permitted work too. As a rule of thumb across the metro: an **attached** patio cover or a **solid-roof** structure needs a permit essentially everywhere, since it carries snow load and ties into the house; a **freestanding open pergola** under a jurisdiction-specific size threshold sometimes doesn't. The thresholds differ by city and county — and HOAs review shade structures regardless. When we build a [pergola or patio cover](/services/pergolas-patio-covers), the permit determination and paperwork are part of the job, same as a deck.

## The HOA wrinkle

Across almost all of our service area, if you're in an HOA you're dealing with **two approvals**: the government building permit and the HOA's architectural review committee. HOAs commonly have rules about deck height, railing style, color, and setback from property lines. Start the HOA submission early — architectural committees sometimes meet only monthly, and that timeline can quietly become the longest part of your project.

## How long does it take?

For a standard residential deck in our area, plan review and permit issuance often runs **one to three weeks**, depending on the jurisdiction's current workload and whether an engineer's stamp is required. HOA approval can add anywhere from a few days to over a month. Build this lead time into your plans — the best time to start permitting is well before the season you actually want to use the deck.
