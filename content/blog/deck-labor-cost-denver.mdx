Homeowners comparing deck bids eventually ask the blunt question: *how much of this is labor?* Fair question. Here's the honest anatomy of a Denver-metro deck quote in 2026 — and why the labor line is the worst place to bargain-hunt.

## The short answer

On a professionally built composite deck in the Denver metro, **labor typically runs 40–50% of the total project cost** — roughly **$15–$35 per square foot** of the $40–$70/sq ft installed price. A 350 sq ft deck carries something like **$6,000–$12,000 of labor**, more if it's elevated, terraced, or detailed with covers and built-ins.

If a bid's labor number is dramatically below that, it isn't a bargain — it's a preview. Something in the schedule, the crew, or the parts of the build you can't see is making up the difference.

## What that labor actually buys

A deck is a small engineered structure attached to your house, and most of the skilled hours go into the parts that disappear from view:

- **Layout and footings.** Digging to Colorado frost depth, setting forms, pouring and anchoring — the foundation phase is slow, heavy, and completely invisible in the finished photos.
- **The ledger.** Attaching and flashing the deck to the house correctly is the single most safety-critical connection on the build. Botched ledgers are the classic cause of deck collapse — and the classic shortcut of cheap labor.
- **Framing.** Beams, joists, blocking, hardware. Board-straightness and hidden-fastener quality on the surface are decided here, one level down.
- **Surface and railings.** The part you see — and at code height and spacing, the railing work is fussier than the field boards.
- **Permits and inspections.** Drawings, submission, scheduling, and standing on site when the inspector shows up. [We handle this end to end](/blog/deck-permits-centennial-arapahoe-douglas); crews that skip it aren't cheaper, they're just transferring risk to you.

## Why Denver labor prices where it does

Colorado's structural requirements are heavier than the national average — frost-depth footings, snow-load framing, wind exposure in the southern metro — and experienced deck carpenters are in demand across a metro that builds outdoor living ten months a year. Skilled crews price accordingly, and the difference shows up twenty years later in a deck that's still square, still tight, and still standing on its original footings.

## How to compare bids on labor

Don't compare the number; compare what it covers. Ask each builder: Who actually swings the hammers — employees or the cheapest available sub? Is the ledger flashed and bolted, or lagged and caulked? Are footings dug to frost depth or set on pads? Is the permit pulled by them or left to you? Same-scope bids in the Denver metro usually land within honest range of each other; a wild outlier means the scopes aren't actually the same.
