---When homeowners get their first deck quote, the surprise is rarely the deck boards. It's the options — the stairs, the railing style, the lighting, the second level — that quietly add thousands to the number. None of it is padding. Each one is real material and real labor. But you can't make good trade-offs if you don't know what each piece costs. So here's a plain-English breakdown of the most common deck add-ons and what they tend to add to a build in the Denver metro in 2026.

## The short version

Here's roughly what the common options add on a typical backyard deck. Ranges depend on size, height, and material — details below.

| Add-on | Typical added cost |
|---|---|
| A run of stairs | $1,500–$4,000 |
| Upgraded railing (over basic composite) | $2,000–$8,000+ |
| Deck lighting (post caps, stair risers, under-rail) | $1,200–$4,500 |
| A second level or tier | $6,000–$20,000+ |
| Built-in benches or planters | $600–$2,500 each |
| Picture-frame border | $800–$2,500 |
| Skirting / lattice below the deck | $1,000–$3,500 |

On most projects, options like these are the difference between the low bid and the high bid on the *same* deck. That's why two quotes for "a 350-square-foot deck" can be $10,000 apart and both be honest.

## Stairs and steps

Stairs are the most underestimated line item on a deck. The cost to build stairs off a deck typically runs **$1,500–$4,000** for a single flight, and the reason is that stairs are their own little structure: footings, stringers, treads, risers, and — critically — a code-compliant handrail on the way down.

What moves the number:

- **Height off the ground.** More vertical rise means more steps, and a tall deck may need a mid-run landing, which is essentially a small deck of its own.
- **Width.** A generous 5- to 6-foot-wide stair reads as an architectural feature and costs more than a narrow builder-minimum run.
- **Wraparound or cascading steps.** Wide, open "waterfall" steps that flow off a corner look fantastic and use a lot more material and cutting than a straight flight.
- **Railing on the stairs.** A graspable stair handrail is code, and the transition between the deck guardrail and the stair rail is fussy, skilled work — it's one of the details that separates a professional install from a weekend one.

Ground-level and near-ground decks that need only a step or two are far cheaper here, which is one reason a low deck stretches a budget further than a walkout.

## Railings — the biggest swing

After deck size, railing is the single largest variable in a quote. Basic composite railing is economical and often close to "included" in a composite deck price. Everything above that is where the number climbs.

| Railing type | Typical installed cost (per linear foot) |
|---|---|
| Composite / basic metal | $30–$60 |
| Aluminum (powder-coated) | $60–$90 |
| Cable rail (stainless) | $80–$130 |
| Glass panel | $100–$150+ |

Run those against real deck perimeters and the swing is large. On a mid-size deck with 60–70 feet of railing, moving from basic composite to cable or glass can add **$4,000–$8,000** on its own.

It's not just looks. On a deck with a foothills or open-space view, **cable and glass** exist to get the pickets out of your sightline — you're paying for the view, not the rail. **Aluminum** is the low-maintenance workhorse that won't rust or need refinishing in our sun. We break down how each system ages in Colorado on our [railings page](/services/railings), and it's worth choosing deliberately, because re-railing later costs more than doing it once.

## What a new stair railing costs on its own

A question we get constantly from people who *aren't* building a new deck: what's the cost for a new stair railing, just by itself? Maybe the old rail is wobbly, maybe an inspector or an insurance appraiser flagged it, maybe it simply looks like 1994.

As a standalone job, new stair railing cost runs roughly **$900–$2,500 for a typical single flight**, installed. The low end is a straight composite or powder-coated aluminum rail on sound stairs; the high end is cable rail, a long or angled run, or stairs whose posts and blocking need rebuilding before a rail can anchor to them — which, on older decks, they often do. Per linear foot, expect stair railing to price **20–40% above** the same railing system on the flat deck, because every piece is cut and fitted on an angle and the code details (graspable profile, 34–38-inch height above the nosings, infill that rejects a 4-inch sphere) are all fussier on a slope.

Two honest notes. First, if the stair structure itself is failing, the railing quote becomes a stair-repair quote — a rail is only as solid as what it bolts to. Second, if the deck's main guardrail is the same vintage, price replacing both at once; the mobilization is shared and the per-foot number drops.

## A second level or a change in elevation

Multi-level decks and tiers are where budgets really move. A second level or a separate tier can add **$6,000–$20,000 or more**, because you're duplicating the expensive, invisible parts — footings, posts, beams, joists — plus the stairs to connect the two, plus more guardrail around more perimeter.

The same logic applies to raising a deck off the ground at all. Every foot of height adds taller posts, deeper structural requirements, guardrail (anything over 30 inches needs it by code), and usually a full run of stairs. This is exactly why a walkout or second-story deck costs meaningfully more than a ground-level one of the same square footage — a point we make in our [full composite deck cost guide](/blog/composite-deck-cost-denver).

## Lighting

Deck lighting is the highest-impact option for the money. Budget roughly **$1,200–$4,500** depending on how much you do:

- **Post-cap lights** — soft ambient light on the railing posts.
- **Recessed stair-riser lights** — the safety upgrade that also looks the most custom.
- **Under-rail and under-bench strips** — a low glow that makes the deck usable after dark.

The key is that lighting is dramatically cheaper to install *during* the build, while the framing is open and wiring can be concealed. Adding it later means fishing wire through a finished deck. If you think you'll ever want it, run the wiring now even if you hold off on some fixtures.

## Built-ins and the finishing details

The smaller options add character and cost in the hundreds-to-low-thousands each:

- **Built-in benches** — $600–$1,800 each, more with hidden storage or a back.
- **Planters** — $500–$1,500 each, integrated into the framing.
- **Picture-frame border** — a perimeter board that hides the cut ends for a finished look; $800–$2,500 depending on deck size and whether it includes a contrasting inlay.
- **Skirting or lattice** below an elevated deck — $1,000–$3,500 to close off (and hide) the space underneath.
- **Fascia and matching trim** — the wrap that makes the framing disappear; usually a few hundred dollars in material but a real difference in the finished look.

None of these are necessary. All of them are the kind of thing people are glad they did — and mildly annoyed they skipped — a year in.

## Code, permits, and why the cheap bid is cheap

Stairs and railings are the most code-regulated parts of a deck, and it's where low bids cut. The riser heights have to match within 3/8 of an inch, treads have a minimum depth, guardrails have to resist a 200-pound load at the top, and stair rails need a graspable profile — not just a flat 2x6 on edge. Front Range building departments inspect all of it, and a stair or rail that fails inspection gets rebuilt, not touched up.

So when one quote for the same stairs is half the other, ask what it includes. Footings under the stringers or just pads on dirt? Metal stringer hangers or toenailed lumber? A listed, tested railing system or shop-made pickets? None of those questions are gotchas — they're the difference between the numbers, and any builder should be happy to answer them line by line.

## How it stacks up: a real example

Take a 350-square-foot capped-composite deck. The base build — a mid-tier board, standard substructure, basic composite railing — might land around **$16,000**. Now add the options a typical homeowner actually wants:

- A wide run of stairs to the yard: **+$3,000**
- Upgrade to aluminum railing for the view: **+$3,500**
- Stair-riser and post-cap lighting: **+$2,500**
- A built-in bench and a picture-frame border: **+$2,000**

That's about **$27,000** — the same deck, roughly 70% more, entirely in options. Neither number is wrong. This is why we itemize every quote: so you can see exactly what each choice costs and decide what's worth it *to you*, rather than discovering it buried in a lump sum.

## The honest bottom line

Options are where a deck becomes *your* deck — and where budgets quietly grow. The two that move the number most are **stairs** and **railing choice**, so decide those first; lighting is the best value per dollar; and anything involving a second level is a structural project, not a trim upgrade. Whatever you're weighing, get it itemized. A bid that spells out each add-on lets you trade a fancier rail for lighting, or skip the skirting to fund the stairs — decisions you can only make when you can see the line items.
