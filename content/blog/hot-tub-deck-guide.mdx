Here's the number that should start every hot-tub-on-a-deck conversation: a typical 6-person spa weighs about 800 pounds empty — and **5,500 to 6,500 pounds filled and occupied**. That's two SUVs, parked on a 7-by-7 footprint, permanently. A standard residential deck is designed for a 40-pounds-per-square-foot live load; a filled tub imposes three times that. Put one on a deck that wasn't engineered for it and the deck will tell you, slowly and then suddenly.

We build hot tub decks regularly across the metro, and the engineering is completely solvable. It just has to be *done*, not assumed.

## The three ways to support a tub

**On grade, deck built around it.** The tub sits on a reinforced concrete pad at ground level, and the deck wraps it — flush with the tub rim or stepping around it. This is the structural freebie: the pad carries the weight, the deck carries people. It's our default recommendation when the yard allows it, and it usually produces the best design too, since a rim-flush tub is far easier to get into than one you climb up to.

**On the deck, engineered for it.** When the tub genuinely needs to be on the deck — walk-outs where grade is a story down, or a layout that demands it — the framing under the tub footprint gets designed for the real load: closer joist spacing, doubled or tripled beams, and additional footings placed directly under the tub zone. This isn't a beefed-up version of normal framing; it's a different structure for that bay, calculated, and it's exactly the kind of thing that goes on the permit drawings.

**On an existing deck — after an honest inspection.** The most dangerous version is the impulse buy: tub delivered Saturday, placed on a 15-year-old deck that was never designed for a fraction of the load. Before a tub goes on any existing deck we inspect the structure the way we would for a [repair assessment](/services/deck-repair) — framing, footings, and ledger — and give you a written answer: fine as-is, fine after reinforcement, or ground-pad time. Reinforcement is often very doable; guessing is not.

## Design details that make or break it

**Access and clearance.** Spa service techs need panel access, usually on a specific side. Plan it during design — a tub serviced by tearing out decking is a tub that never gets serviced.

**Rim height.** Sunken or semi-sunken installs (deck at rim height) look best and enter easiest, but need drainage and airflow in the vault so the tub isn't sitting in a humid pit. We frame vault walls with ventilation and a drainage path, always.

**Privacy and wind.** Colorado hot-tubbing is a winter sport, and wind is the enemy of a 104-degree soak. Slat [privacy screens](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) and partial roofs turn an exposed tub into a year-round one — some of our favorite builds pair a tub vault with a louvered pergola overhead.

**Power and code.** A spa circuit is a 220V/50-amp run with its own disconnect at code-mandated distance. We coordinate the electrical rough-in during framing so nothing gets trenched through a finished yard later.

**Surface choice.** Wet feet, dripped chlorine, and constant splash-zone moisture argue hard for [capped composite or PVC](/services/composite-decks) over wood around a tub — no refinishing a splash zone every summer, no splinters on bare feet.

## What about pool decks?

Same physics, different scale. We build composite decks and surrounds for above-ground pools and spas — the wrap-around platform that turns an above-ground pool from an appliance into a place. In-ground pool surrounds in concrete or pavers are a different trade (our [concrete vs. pavers guide](/blog/concrete-vs-pavers-guide) covers that decision), but where the answer is a raised wood or composite structure, that's deck work, and it gets the same engineering attention as a tub vault: real footings, moisture-immune surfaces, and framing sized for wet loads.

## What it costs

A hot-tub-ready deck carries a premium only in the reinforced zone — figure **$1,500–$4,000 above standard framing** for the engineered bay, footings, and vault work, on top of normal deck pricing of $40–$70 per square foot installed. A ground-pad install is cheaper still: the pad typically runs $1,000–$2,500 and the deck around it prices as a normal deck. Electrical runs are separate and site-dependent.
