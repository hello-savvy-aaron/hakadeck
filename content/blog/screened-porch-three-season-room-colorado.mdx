Somewhere between "deck" and "addition" lives the space most Colorado homeowners actually want: outdoor enough to feel like outside, sheltered enough to use in April and October, and bug-proof enough that a summer evening doesn't end at dusk. That space has two versions — the screened porch and the three-season room — and as a custom porch builder we spend a lot of consultations explaining the difference, because the price gap is real and the right answer depends on how you'll actually use it.

## The screened porch: airflow with a roof

A screened porch is a roofed outdoor room with screen walls — full airflow, full outdoor feeling, zero mosquitoes. The roof is a real roof (we build [tongue-and-groove and polycarbonate pavilion roofs](/services/pergolas-patio-covers), flashed and pitched properly), the floor is usually deck or patio surface, and the walls are framed screen panels.

What it gives you: bug-free evenings, shade, rain shelter, and a space that reads as porch rather than room. What it doesn't: warmth. A screened porch in January is January with a nice view. In our climate that makes it a genuine three-season space on the calendar — reliably comfortable from roughly April into October, plus every warm winter afternoon Colorado hands out for free.

**Cost picture:** screening in an existing covered deck or patio typically runs **$3,000–$8,000** depending on span and panel systems. Building the whole thing — roof structure and screens over an existing deck — lands most projects at **$15,000–$35,000**. From bare ground, add normal deck or patio pricing underneath.

## The three-season room: glass instead of screens

A three-season room trades screen panels for windows — usually big sliding or stacking glass — over the same kind of structure. The glass cuts wind entirely, traps solar warmth on sunny days, and stretches the usable calendar by a month or more on each end. With Colorado's 300-plus days of sun, a south-facing glass room can be genuinely pleasant on a 40-degree February afternoon with no heat source but the sun.

What it isn't: a four-season addition. Three-season rooms are unconditioned and typically single-glazed — they aren't insulated living space, don't need to meet the energy code an addition does, and cost dramatically less as a result. That's the entire point: perhaps 80% of an addition's usefulness at a fraction of its cost and paperwork.

**Cost picture:** converting a screened porch to three-season glass typically runs **$8,000–$20,000** on top of the porch, driven mostly by the window system. Purpose-built from an existing deck, most land between **$25,000 and $50,000** — still well under the cost of conditioned square footage.

## The smart move: build the porch so it can become the room

Here's the advice that saves people the most money: if a three-season room is where you might end up, tell your builder before the porch goes up. The upgrade path is cheap when it's planned — framing sized and spaced to accept window units later, a header and sill detail that takes glass, electrical roughed for future baseboard heat — and expensive when it's not. We design most of our screened porches this way by default; the framing costs almost nothing extra, and it keeps a $15k decision from foreclosing a $30k one.

The same logic runs down the whole chain, which is why we always ask what the five-year vision is. Deck → [covered deck](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) → screened porch → three-season room is a ladder, and every rung is much cheaper to climb if the rung below was built expecting it. Footings that can carry a roof, a roof that can carry glass.

## Colorado specifics worth knowing

**Snow load rules the roof.** Whatever the walls are made of, the roof over them is a structural roof in a snow state — engineered, permitted, and inspected. This is the line item bargain bids quietly undersize, and our [pergola and cover engineering](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) treats it as non-negotiable.

**Wind is why screens beat open air here.** On the Front Range's exposed lots, an open deck can be unusable on a gusty afternoon when a screened porch is perfectly calm. Screens knock wind down remarkably well while keeping the airflow that makes a porch feel like a porch.

**Permits and HOAs apply.** A roofed, walled structure is real construction to every metro jurisdiction and most HOAs. We handle [the drawings, permits, and design review](/deck-permits-south-denver-metro) end to end, same as any build.

## Which one is right for you?

Rough test: if the sentence is "I want to sit outside without bugs," build the screened porch. If it's "I want to use it in March," build the three-season room — or build the porch with the upgrade path designed in and decide after a year of living with it. Either way the structure underneath should be built once, correctly, expecting its future.
