A cover is the single upgrade that changes how much a Colorado deck actually gets used — shade in July, shelter in an afternoon storm, and a season that runs from March snow-showers into November. It's also where quotes diverge most wildly, because "covered deck" spans everything from an open pergola to a fully roofed outdoor room. Here are the real 2026 numbers for the Denver metro, and the engineering behind why they are what they are.

## The short answer, by roof type

For a cover added to a new or structurally sound existing deck:

| Cover type | Typical installed cost |
|---|---|
| Open-rafter pergola (timber or aluminum) | $6,000–$15,000 |
| Adjustable louvered-roof pergola | $15,000–$30,000+ |
| Polycarbonate roof cover | $10,000–$22,000 |
| Solid roof / tongue-and-groove pavilion | $15,000–$35,000 |
| Screened enclosure (added to a covered structure) | +$3,000–$8,000 |

Building deck and cover together? Add normal deck pricing — [$40–$70 per square foot installed](/blog/composite-deck-cost-denver) for capped composite — and plan the two as one structure, which is both cheaper and better-engineered than bolting a roof onto a deck that wasn't expecting one.

## What moves the number

**Span and posts.** A roof that clears a 12-foot deck with two posts is a different structure than one spanning 20 feet with an open corner. Longer spans mean bigger beams — aluminum spans farther than timber before needing a post — and every post means a footing.

**The snow-load line.** This is the quiet difference between a Denver quote and an internet price. A flatland pergola kit is designed for sun; a Front Range cover is designed for the wet spring dump that lands two feet of heavy snow on it — and for the wind that tries to lift it the rest of the year. Real engineering means deeper footings, heavier connections, and beams sized for loads a kit never contemplated. It's why our covers cost more than the box-store version, and why ours are [still standing after the storms](/services/pergolas-patio-covers) that flatten the kits.

**Roof material.** Open rafters are the least material and the least shelter. Multiwall polycarbonate lets daylight through while blocking UV and shrugging off hail — our [honest guide to polycarbonate covers](/blog/polycarbonate-roof-covers-guide) covers the panel grades. Tongue-and-groove wood ceilings under a solid roof give an indoor-quality finish overhead and the full outdoor-room effect; they're the top of the range for good reason.

**What's underneath.** A roof adds real load, so an existing deck gets a structural check first — sometimes reinforcement, occasionally the honest news that the deck itself is the project. Our [10-minute inspection guide](/deck-safety-inspection-guide) will give you a preview of what we'll look at.

**Electrical.** Lights, ceiling fans, and infrared heaters are what turn a cover into a room. Wired during the build they're a modest line; retrofitted later they're an ordeal. Budget $800–$2,500 for a lighting-and-fan package, more with heaters.

## The use-it math

Here's the way we'd actually think about the spend. An uncovered west-facing deck in the metro is genuinely comfortable maybe four hours a day in summer — mornings and after sunset. The same deck under shade is usable all day, and under a solid roof it's usable in rain, hail season, and shoulder-month weather. Homeowners consistently tell us the cover doubled their real use of the space. On a $25,000 deck, a $15,000 cover that doubles usage is the cheapest square footage you'll ever add — which is why "cover now or later?" is the question we ask during every design.

If later is the honest answer for the budget, say so during design: we'll place footings and size framing so the roof can land in two years without rework. Phasing is a [financing strategy](/financing) in its own right, and it beats both waiting and over-borrowing.
