---When homeowners have decided on composite, the next question is always *which brand?* Trex, TimberTech, and Fiberon are the three names most people have heard of, and every one of them makes a genuinely good board in 2026. But they're not interchangeable — they differ on heat, structure, looks, and warranty in ways that matter, especially at 5,400 feet under Colorado sun. Here's how we actually think about them as the people who install them.

## The 30-second version

- **Trex** — the household name. Widest availability, huge color range, strong value at the entry tier. A safe, proven choice.
- **TimberTech** — the premium pick. Best-in-class capping and the deepest realistic wood looks, including a true PVC line (AZEK) that's the most weather-proof of the group.
- **Fiberon** — the value play. Genuinely good boards, often a little less money than the equivalent Trex or TimberTech line.

There's no wrong answer among the three. There's a *right* answer for your budget, your color taste, and how much sun your deck takes.

## Heat: the one that matters most in Colorado

At altitude, with 300-plus sunny days a year, surface temperature is the first thing we think about — a south- or west-facing deck can get uncomfortable barefoot in July if you pick the wrong board and color.

All three brands have addressed this, but not equally:

- **TimberTech** leads here. Its capped polymer (AZEK) and CoolDeck-technology capstock run meaningfully cooler than standard composite, and the difference is real underfoot.
- **Trex** answered with its Transcend Lineage line, engineered to stay cooler than its older dark boards. Its entry lines (Enhance) still get hot in dark colors.
- **Fiberon** offers cooler-surface options in its premium lines but less consistently across the range.

The rule that beats brand loyalty: **on a sunny, unshaded deck, pick a lighter color and a cooler-surface line — whichever brand it's in.** A dark standard board bakes no matter whose name is on it.

## Structure: capped composite vs. PVC

Two of these brands sell fundamentally different board *types*, and it's worth understanding:

- **Capped composite** (most Trex, most TimberTech PRO/EDGE, most Fiberon) is a wood-and-plastic core wrapped in a polymer cap. Excellent for the vast majority of decks.
- **PVC / cellular vinyl** (TimberTech AZEK) has no wood content at all. It's lighter, the most moisture- and heat-resistant of the group, and the top choice for ground-level, poolside, or heavy-freeze-thaw applications. It also costs the most.

For a standard elevated backyard deck, capped composite from any of the three is the right tool. For a low deck close to the ground, or a client who wants the absolute longest-lived surface, TimberTech AZEK earns its premium.

## Looks

This is genuinely subjective, but our general read:

- **TimberTech** has the most convincing wood grain and multi-tonal streaking — from six feet away, its premium lines are hard to tell from stained hardwood.
- **Trex** has the widest color palette, which matters if you're matching a specific stone, stucco, or trim color.
- **Fiberon** looks great and is often the closest premium look for the money.

The honest advice: don't choose from a website. Get physical sample boards, set them on your actual deck site, and look at them in your own light — morning and evening, sun and shade. Color reads completely differently outdoors than on a screen or in a showroom.

## Warranty

All three offer strong, transferable warranties on their capped and PVC lines — typically **25 to 50 years** on fade and stain, with the premium/PVC lines at the top end. On paper they're close. In practice, a warranty is only as good as the installation: nearly every real-world composite failure we've seen traces back to framing, drainage, or fastening — not the board. Which brings us to the part that matters more than any brand.

## The part nobody markets: installation

Here's the honest truth from the install side — **the brand you pick matters less than who frames the deck.** A top-tier board on a substructure with footings above the frost line, undersized joist spacing, or the wrong fasteners will fail long before its warranty runs out, and the manufacturer won't cover framing mistakes. A mid-tier board on a properly engineered, correctly-flashed, freeze-thaw-ready structure will outlast it easily.

So when you're comparing bids, spend less energy agonizing over Trex-vs-TimberTech and more on the substructure spec, footing depth, and the builder's own labor warranty. ## How we choose on a real project

Our default framework:

1. **Sunny, unshaded, want it cool underfoot?** TimberTech premium (AZEK or a CoolDeck capstock line).
2. **Want a specific color or the broadest palette at a fair price?** Trex.
3. **Great composite deck on a tighter budget?** Fiberon.
4. **Low, ground-level, or poolside deck?** TimberTech AZEK PVC.
5. **Not sure?** We're a Deckorators Pro Elite installer, and for many Colorado yards its mineral-based boards — cooler and more dimensionally stable through big temperature swings — are our actual first recommendation. Worth putting on the sample table alongside the big three.

## Bottom line

However you order the matchup — Trex vs. TimberTech, TimberTech vs. Fiberon, or Fiberon vs. Trex vs. TimberTech straight across — it's decided by the same three questions: how much sun the deck takes, whether the site calls for capped composite or PVC, and what the budget honestly is. Trex, TimberTech, and Fiberon all make boards we'd happily install. Pick TimberTech for premium looks and the coolest, most weatherproof surface; Trex for value and color range; Fiberon for the best composite dollar-for-dollar. Then — more importantly — pick a builder who frames it for Colorado.
